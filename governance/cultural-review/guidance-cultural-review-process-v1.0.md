## AIWA CULTURAL REVIEW PROCESS

---

## 1. Purpose

AIWA operates in a culturally rich and diverse region. The Gambia is home to multiple
ethnic communities — including Mandinka, Fula, Wolof, Jola, Serer, Sarahule, and others —
each with their own traditions, languages, oral histories, and cultural practices.

This Cultural Review Process ensures that:

- AIWA's materials treat all Gambian cultural heritage with respect and accuracy
- Traditional and communal knowledge is not misrepresented, misappropriated, or
  falsely attributed as individual creation
- Community voices are included in the review of culturally sensitive materials

---

## 2. When Cultural Review Is Required

Cultural Review must be conducted for any AIWA document, publication, or programme
material that:

- References or quotes traditional folklore, oral histories, proverbs, or songs
- Depicts cultural practices, ceremonies, or community traditions
- Is intended for use across multiple ethnic communities and must be culturally appropriate
  for all groups
- Could be perceived as making claims about traditional cultural heritage

---

## 3. The Distinction Between Modern Works and Traditional Heritage

This is a critical distinction for AIWA's publishing and programme work.

### Modern Student Contributor-Created Works
Original stories, essays, and poems written by students or teachers are individual
creative works. They are owned by their creators and governed by publishing agreements.

Even where a modern work is *inspired by* a traditional story or theme, the modern work
is the creator's own. However, any traditional elements must be acknowledged clearly.

### Traditional and Communal Cultural Heritage
Oral traditions, folklore, proverbs, ceremonies, and communal knowledge that have been
passed down through generations within Gambian communities are **collective heritage**.
They cannot be owned by any individual or by AIWA.

If an AIWA publication includes a traditional tale or proverb, it must be:
- Clearly labelled as traditional/communal heritage
- Attributed to the community of origin (e.g., *"A traditional Mandinka story"*)
- Reviewed by a community representative or cultural consultant

---

## 4. Review Process

### Step 1: Identify
The document owner identifies content that may require cultural review.

### Step 2: Consult
Engage a community representative, cultural consultant, or relevant expert to review
the content for accuracy, appropriateness, and correct attribution.

### Step 3: Document
Record the review outcome in the Cultural Review Log (see `/governance/cultural-review/`).

### Step 4: Approve
The Programme Director approves the material for use, noting the cultural review completed.
